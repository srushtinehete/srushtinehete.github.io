The Athenaeum was a library founded by Emperor Hadrian in Rome, for the promotion of literary and scientific studies. But it was more than that. The Athenaeum was space for the community to gather, celebrate, interact and protest. Our internet archive re-envisions the Athenaeum in today’s digital age.

 

The design approach to this urban site reflects the urban fabric of the city of Toronto. The existing city grid has been brought into the site to maintain the major axes. A mound has been created on the north of the site to act as a buffer between the traffic, at the same time, it represents Canadian landscape and cottage culture. The berm is injected with trails and biking paths that break the grid and provides for recreational spaces like an amphitheatre, a sculpture park and a ‘backyard’ for outdoor activities. Piers and wading pools jut in and out of the edge of the lake, along with the grid, similar to the extended lakeshore along the entire city.

The building itself is a ‘modernist’ box from the outside, but as you enter it, all hell breaks loose. Organic circulation patterns define activities and spaces. Two large oculi on the interior bring in light while dividing different programs. One remains an outdoor space with a small indoor garden, while the other provides for physical as well as visual access from one floor to the other. Volumetric expression of the roof creates a unique icon for the city to identify its archive, while the column-free shell space on the first floor allows for uninterrupted views and large gatherings.

Essentially a library and archiving facility, the building aims to understand how digital technology changes social practice. Our site, Toronto, is one of the most culturally diverse cities in Canada and the world. It caters to a diverse economic and ethnic population, along with being a popular tourist centre. The digital athenaeum provides for large event spaces, both indoors and outdoors to provide for a wide variety of activities. The program also recognizes the need for additional tools for the public that a library must provide, for example, maker labs, access to expensive equipment, film studios and digital working spaces for the rising freelance and start-up generation. This together with a traditional program of the library creates public space that sparks a dialogue about the importance of libraries and archives in today’s digital age.
